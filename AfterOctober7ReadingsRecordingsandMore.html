After October 7:

Readings, Recordings, and More

Document-in-Progress (Latest Update: February 25, 2025)

Curated by Erika Dreifus (Ed.M, M.F.A., Ph.D)

Prefatory notes: This document does not aim to be comprehensive. Its purpose is both personal—to record many of my own encounters with texts and other materials following the launch of Hamas’s terrorist attack on southern Israel the morning of October 7[1]—and educational. As an academically trained historian, educator, and multi-genre American Jewish writer who happens to possess more than a passing familiarity with Israeli history, literature and culture, I’m offering this document as an informed resource.

My overall (but not exclusive) emphasis here is on amplifying Jewish perspectives—including writers’ perspectives—from Israel and the Jewish Diaspora that I’ve found especially resonant and/or clarifying. I’ve made this my priority in part due to longstanding currents in the academic, literary, and adjacent communities with which I am most familiar. At best, such patterns erase Jewish voices that do not adhere to a prevalent (if, for Jewish communities beyond those bubbles, a decidedly minority/fringe) anti-Israelist ethos. Far more dangerously, they amplify increasingly visible and virulent antisemitic statements and tropes. (For examples, please check here.)

Please check back for additions and updates. Thank you for reading.

Please note: I cannot guarantee access to (semi-)paywalled materials. If you encounter difficulties accessing anything here, please consider these tips: 1) try another browser; 2) wait for a new week/month to try again; 3) request assistance from your local library.

Go-To Resources

  • For (non-paywalled) news coverage in English: The Times of Israel (and their “Daily Briefing” podcast, which unfortunately does not offer transcripts, but does provide helpful show notes); Jewish Insider (for best access, I recommend subscribing to the free “Daily Kickoff” newsletter); JTA.
  • The websites of both the American Jewish Committee and the Anti-Defamation League provide essential information hubs for education, advocacy, and more. The Israel Policy Forum’s Israel at War Resource Hub similarly offers a compilation of “timely content, including a comprehensive timeline of the Israel-Hamas conflict, articles, infographics, podcasts, videos, and more.”
  • Rabbi Toby Manewith’s “Is It Antisemitism? If It Is, Now What? Guidance for a Post-October 7th World,” on the website of the Religious Action Center of Reform Judaism, contains helpful explanations and constructive practical suggestions, including specific advice for high schoolers and collegians.
  • The Shalom Hartman Institute’s “For Heaven’s Sake” podcast has upped its frequency for a special series of “Israel at War” episodes featuring conversations between Jerusalem-based Donniel Hartman and Yossi Klein Halevi. Transcripts available.
  • Since October 7, I have been unable to keep up with a number of Israel-focused sources that I normally rely on. These include Daniel Gordis’s Israel from the Inside, Vivian Bercovici’s State of Tel Aviv, and, again, Israel Policy Forum. I’ve also yet to listen to each and every “Wartime Diaries” mini-episode from the Israel Story podcast. In the meantime, I’ve also become increasingly reliant on Dan Senor’s Call Me Back. (Please note: Not all podcasts seem to include transcripts.)
  • The Association of Jewish Libraries has updated its “Love Your Neighbor” book list focused on Israel.
  • Unfortunately, developments have been such that the Jewish Book Council has launched a mechanism for “Reporting Antisemitism in the Literary World.”
